The Ghost in the Machine: Why Cross-Account IAM Roles are the Silent Scope-Creepers of AWS Audits

In a traditional data center, you audit the users on the server. In AWS, that approach will fail you. Cross-account IAM roles are the industry standard for managing access at scale, but they are also the #1 reason audits unintentionally under-scope.

If your Account A (Production) trusts Account B (Shared Services or Dev), your audit boundary does not stop at the Account A login screen. It extends deep into Account B's governance.

If you are not looking at who can assume those roles, you are not actually auditing access - you are just looking at a list of names while the real actors are coming through the side door.

The Technical "Handshake": How it Actually Works

To audit this effectively, you must understand that cross-account access is not a single permission; it is a two-way handshake. If one side is missing, access fails. If both are present, your audit scope just expanded.

Cross-account access requires both a trust policy in the destination account and an identity policy in the source account.

1. The Trust Policy (The "Lock")

This lives in the Destination Account (for example, your sensitive Production account). It defines which external entities are allowed to "knock on the door."

  • What to look for: A JSON block called the Principal.
  • The Auditor's Warning: If the Principal is arn:aws:iam::123456789012:root, the account has effectively handed a master key to the entirety of the other account. This is a common finding that DevOps teams often overlook in the name of "simplicity."

2. The Identity Policy (The "Key")

This lives in the Source Account (for example, a Management or Dev account). It grants a specific user or service the permission to perform the sts:AssumeRole action.

  • What to look for: A policy that points to the Amazon Resource Name (ARN) of the role in the destination account.
The Auditor's Rule of Thumb: Access is defined by who can assume a role, not by who "exists" in the account.

3 Common Blind Spots That Kill Audit Accuracy

1. The "No IAM Users" Trap (SSO/Federation)

Many GRC teams claim an account is "low risk" because it has zero IAM users. In modern environments, users live in Okta, Azure AD, or AWS IAM Identity Center. They authenticate into a "Hub" account and then "hop" into the Production account. If your evidence request only asks for a list of IAM users in Production, you are missing 100% of the actual human activity.

2. The CI/CD "Backdoor"

Your GitHub Actions, GitLab runners, or Jenkins servers often live in a "Shared Services" account. They use roles to push code to Production. From an audit perspective, that CI/CD pipeline is a privileged user. If a junior developer in the "Dev" account can modify that pipeline, they effectively have an administrative path into Production.

3. Role Chaining (Transitive Access)

AWS allows "Role Chaining," where User A assumes Role B, which then assumes Role C. Auditors often stop at the first hop. To ensure a complete audit, you must follow the chain until you reach the actual human or machine identity that started the process.

How to Adjust Your Audit Program

If you want to move beyond "check-the-box" cloud auditing, update your evidence requests with these three steps:

  • Follow the Trust: Do not just ask for a list of users. Ask for the Trust Relationships of all roles with AdministratorAccess or similar, or data-layer permissions (S3, RDS, DynamoDB).
  • Validate the Origin: If a role is assumed from another account, you must review the IAM policies in that source account to see which identities are authorized to call sts:AssumeRole.
  • Audit the IdP: If the organization uses SSO, the "User List" for the Production account actually lives in the Identity Provider (Okta/Azure). That is where your "Source of Truth" for joiner/mover/leaver reviews must reside.
